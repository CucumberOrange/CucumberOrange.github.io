Arsenic

Properties & uses

Arsenic is considered a heavy metal, with an atomic mass of 74.9 amu. Unlike other members of the metalloid group, Arsenic has two solid forms: a reactive, unstable yellow nonmetal, and a steel-gray brittle metal, the more common form. Although gray Arsenic remains stable in both water and dry air, it will tarnish in the presence of moist air, oxidizing to form Arsenic Trioxide. Arsenic has no liquid form, and sublimes directly into a gas at 613 oC, where it reacts with the air to form Arsenous Oxide. Uses for this element include wood preservation and the manufacture of special glass. In very low doses, Arsenic is an essential part of the diet, which is taken in through consumption of seafood, rice, mushrooms and even poultry, whose feed is sprinkled with Arsenic to improve weight gain and meat yield.

Most victims come in contact with this poisonous element through groundwater contaminated by Arsenic in the Earth’s mantle or more commonly, runoff waste from industrial plants. Other causes include smoking tobacco and eating food with high Arsenic levels, such as large fish whose Arsenic levels rise through bioaccumulation. Early symptoms include swollen skin, acute vomiting, chronic brain degeneration and diarrhea accompanied by blood. According to the World Health Organization, symptoms of long term Arsenic poisoning first make themselves known in the skin, in the form of hyperpigmentation and even skin cancer, as this element is considered to be a carcinogen. Cancer also occurs in the major organs, such as the kidneys and the liver. Other symptoms include numbness, heart disease and death. Calcium Arsenate (Ca3(AsO4)2) and Lead (II) Hydrogen Arsenate (PbHArO4), are the compounds of choice when it comes to poisoning, because they are both soluble in water, and more importantly, Arsenate (AsO4)3-is an analog of Phosphate(PO4)3-, which allows it to disrupt cellular processes essential for life, such as Glycolysis..
